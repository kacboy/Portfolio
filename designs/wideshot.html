Summary

This is a senior publishing course project where each student had to create an original magazine brand and design. I took this course remotely in Summer 2020 so we didn't actually have to print any pages. I took this as a chance to be more creative with my layouts and made a more unconventionally sized magazine, since I didn't need to worry about printing a unique size of page.



Editorial Mandate

Wideshot would cover independent, hobbyist, and student film related topics. The tone should be casual, educational, and inspiring. The magazine would be split into three sections with different content focuses:


Pre-production
This section would cover topics like screenwriting, creative techniques, equipment, and other valuable knowledge pieces that would help a reader before the cameras are rolling.


Principle photography
This section would be the draw of the magazine around the gutter, containing interviews, previews, and long form features.


Post-production
This section would contain reviews of notable indie films, post-mortems, highlights of student films, opinion pieces, and other other reader driven content such as film competitions.


Design Rationale

The name Wideshot is a term unique to filmmaking, photography, and cinematography that is both specific and creative in itself. A wideshot could capture an entire scene, a location, or a character. This term is a clear-cut way of portraying the magazine’s broad coverage of the filmic mediums.


The size of the cover and magazine is based on the industry standard CinemaScope film aspect ratio of 2.39:1. A full spread of the magazine is 20”x8.5” (10”x8.5” pages) to stay within common printable sizes. The typeface and logo was chosen because of it’s modern sans-serif style and the thick, wide characters would allow for an effective knock-out effect with the cover image. The space for the cover image between the two white ‘letterboxes’ is 16:9, another industry standard aspect ratio. The cover image was chosen because of it’s bold color and strong sense of emotion. The cover lines were made very minimal and uniform because I didn’t ever want to intrude over the images on the cover or in the magazine to preserve there intended visual qualities.


For the feature spread I wanted the images to be the forefront of each page and to not ever be covered by text or distracting graphics. I used the images and line elements as the anchors of each spread, and tried not to overflow any page with too much text.
